  • Material is removed from target by momentum transfer

    • Sputtered atoms condense on the substrate

Magnetron sputtering

In the magnetron sputtering, permanent magnets are placed behind the sputtering target to increase the Ar ion density in front of it:
  • The magnetic field forces the free electrons into helical paths around its magnetic lines.
  • This extends the electron path to the anode, thereby extending the time during which they can ionize Argon atoms.
The generated Ar ions are barely affected by the magnetic field because of their weight and they only accelerate in the direction of target, which they strike and remove material through sputtering.

Process conditions

  • Heavy inert gas is the major carrier, e.g. Ar.
  • Reactive chemical species (e.g. \( O_2 \) to deposit oxide) may be introduced - reactive sputtering.
  • Sputtering process can be run in DC or RF mode (insulator must be run in RF mode).
  • Major process parameters:
    • Operation pressure (~10-100mTorr)
    • Power (few 100W)
    • Substrate bias (in addition to self-bias)
    • Substrate temperature (20-850°C)

Sputter Yield

Sputter yield: the number of sputtered atoms per incident ion
  • Energy of each incoming ion is 500-1000eV. Energy of sputtered atoms is 3-10eV. Sputter yield is 1-3.
  • Thus, 95% of incoming energy goes to target heating & secondary electron.
  • High rate sputter processes need efficient cooling techniques to avoid target damage from overheating.

Sputtering yield

  • Determines the deposition rate (higher yield → higher deposition rate)
    Depends on:
    • Mass of bombarding ions.
    • Energy of the bombarding ions (ion energy↑, sputtering yield↑)
    • Direction of incidence of ions (angle).
    • Pressure.

Energy of the bombarding ions

Direction

There is a probability that atom C will be ejected from the surface as a result of the surface being stuck by atom A.
In oblique angle (45°-90°) there is higher probability for sputtering, which occur closer to the surface.

Sputtering methods

  • DC sputtering
  • RF Sputtering
  • Reactive sputtering

DC Sputtering

Deposition parameters:
  • Process pressure: compromise between the number of Ar ions and the scattering of Ar ions with neutral Ar atoms
  • Sputter voltage: maximum yield, typical -2 to -5 kV, for high deposition rate.
  • Substrate bias: control ion bombardment characteristics.
  • Substrate temperature: modify deposited film properties.
  • For conducting materials only.
Operating Pressure for DC sputtering
Operating pressure limitations are imposed by the requirement of both the glow discharge and of film deposition.
Parameters for DC Sputtering
  • Substrate Bias Voltage - substrate is being bombarded by electrons and ions from target and plasma; sputtering film while you deposit; neutral atoms deposit independently; put negative bias on the substrate to control this; can significantly change film properties
  • Deposition rate
  • Changes with Ar pressure
  • Increases with sputter yield
  • Usually increases with high voltage

RF Sputtering

Good for insulating materials because positive charge builds up on the cathode (target) in DC sputtering systems. Alternating potential can avoid charge buildup.
Need \( 10^{12} \) volts to DC sputter insulators !!
  • When frequencies less than about 50kHz, electrons and ions in plasma are mobile - DC sputtering of both surfaces
  • When frequencies above about 50kHz, ions (heavy) can no longer follow the switching - Electrons can neutralize positive charge build up.
13.56 MHz RF source
In RF sputtering a cathode (the target) and an anode, in series with a blocking capacitor (C).
The capacitor C is placed in the circuit to develop the all-important DC self-bias, and a matching network is utilized to optimize power transfer from the RF source to the plasma.
The power supply is a high voltage RF source fixed at 13.56 MHz.
Advantages of RF Sputtering:
  • It works well with insulating targets
  • High efficiency - easier to keep plasma going → can operate at lower Ar pressures (1-15 mTorr) - fewer gas collisions → more line of sight deposition

Reactive Sputtering

Sputtering metallic target in the presence of a reactive gas mixed with inert gas (Ar)
  • oxides - \( \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3, \text{SiO}_2, \text{Ta}_2\text{O}_5 (\text{O}_2) \)
  • nitrides - \( \text{TaN}, \text{TiN}, \text{Si}_3\text{N}_4 (\text{N}_2, \text{NH}_3) \)
  • carbides - \( \text{TiC}, \text{WC}, \text{SiC} (\text{CH}_4, \text{C}_2\text{H}_4, \text{C}_3\text{H}_8) \)
Chemical reaction takes place on substrate and target
  • Can poison target if chemical reactions are faster than sputter rate
  • Adjust reactive gas flow to get good stoichiometry without incorporating excess gas into film
Ex: SnO Preparation
Sputtering of Sn metal in the gas mixture of Ar and \( \text{O}_2 \) (reactive gas)
\( \text{Cu}_3\text{N} \) preparation
Sputtering of Cu metal in the gas mixture of Ar and reactive gas \( \text{N}_2 \)

Advantages of sputter deposition

  • The sputtering target provides a stable, long-lived vaporization source.
  • The source and substrate can be spaced close together.
  • The sputter deposition chamber can have a small volume.
  • Able to deposit a wide variety of metals, insulators and composites.
  • Replication of target composition in the deposited films.
  • Capable of in-situ cleaning prior to film deposition by reversing the potential on the electrodes.
  • Better film quality and step coverage than evaporation, preferred deposition technique for micro-fabrication (semiconductor industry...)

Disadvantages of Sputter Deposition

  • Sputtering targets are often expensive
  • Most of the energy incident on the target becomes heat, which must be removed
  • In reactive sputter deposition, the gas composition must be carefully controlled to prevent poisoning the sputtering target
  • Slow deposition process compared to evaporator
