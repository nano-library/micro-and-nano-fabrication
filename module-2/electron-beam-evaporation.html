A hot filament causes the thermionic emission of electrons, which can, after acceleration, provide sufficient energy for evaporating any material.
Electrons accelerated through a 10 kV voltage drop, 10 kW is delivered upon impact to source material
Using a focused electron beam to heat and evaporate metals, electron temperature can be as high as 10,000 K.
Suitable for high \( T_{\text{meit}} \) metals like \(W\), \(Ta\), \(\ldots\)
Evaporation occurs at a highly localized point on the source surface, so little contamination from the crucible.
Composite films can be deposited using dual e-beams with dual targets

E-beam crucibles

  • Coated with liners to provide better thermal insulation between source and water coolant.
  • Crucibles must be of good thermal conductive.

Limitations

  • Electron Radiation causes damage to materials like polymers
  • Highly directional, not good for large area depositions

Considerations of evaporation technique

  • Deposition rate.
  • Temperature: some metals requires excessive high temperature.
  • Contamination control.
  • Residual stresses: usually of CTE and micro-structures transformation. (CTE: coefficient of thermal expansion)
  • Evaporation is the most useful thin film deposit techniques for nanofabrication, because:
    • It is directional (poor step coverage), good for liftoff.
    • It is directional, good for shadow evaporation.
    • The film thickness can be monitored precisely using a quartz balance.
  • Best suitable for elemental materials (metals, silicon, Ge...), not so good for composite (SiO\(_2\) → SiO\(_x\)). Sputtering is suitable for deposition of composite materials.
